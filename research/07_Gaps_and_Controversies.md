# 07 — Gaps and Controversies

## 1) Weighted-ball training: efficacy vs safety tension
- **What we know:** often improves velocity/arm-speed metrics in many settings.
- **What remains uncertain:** dose-response and injury risk thresholds across ages and workloads.
- **Why controversy persists:** programs differ massively (ball weights, volume, intent, supervision, concurrent lifting/throwing).

## 2) In-season lifting dosage for pitchers by role
- Strong consensus to continue lifting in-season, but
- Weak direct evidence on exact set/rep/frequency targets by starter vs reliever vs two-way player.

## 3) Transfer problem: gym strength to mound velocity
- Some athletes gain strength with little velocity transfer.
- Transfer likely depends on movement constraints, timing, intent exposure, and throwing program architecture.

## 4) Youth and high school risk management
- Adolescents often use advanced methods (weighted balls/high intent) before foundational tissue capacity and load literacy are built.
- Need better age- and maturation-specific protocols.

## 5) Measurement fragmentation
- Teams/facilities track different metrics (velo, command proxies, force metrics, wellness, pain, ROM), making cross-study comparisons difficult.

## 6) Publication gap in private performance sectors
- Elite private labs generate large internal datasets but limited peer-reviewed publication.
- This creates evidence asymmetry: lots of outcomes reported publicly, fewer controlled analyses.

## 7) Contradictory schools of thought
- **Conservative medical view:** caution on high-intent/weighted methods due to tissue stress concerns.
- **Aggressive performance view:** controlled high-intent exposure is essential for velocity adaptation.
- **Balanced view (most defensible):** high intent is useful when nested inside strict workload and recovery governance.

## Priority research needs
1. Multi-center prospective trials comparing in-season lifting models in pitchers.
2. Weighted-ball protocols with standardized progression and injury surveillance.
3. Age/maturation-stratified velocity and injury outcomes.
4. Better integration studies: throwing workload + lifting dose + recovery behavior.
