# 03 — Off-Season Programming (Pitchers)

## Off-season priorities
1. **Raise force ceiling** (max strength)
2. **Translate to high-speed output** (power, ballistic intent, med-ball, sprint/jump)
3. **Increase tissue tolerance** (cuff, forearm-flexor/pronator, scapular musculature, trunk/hip)
4. **Build robust movement options** (thoracic/hip/shoulder mobility + control)

## Recommended phase structure (16–24 weeks)

### Phase 1: Rebuild / General Prep (4–6 weeks)
- Moderate loads, moderate volume
- Movement quality and asymmetry cleanup
- Aerobic base + work capacity (not excessive long slow volume)
- Begin cuff/scap progression

### Phase 2: Max Strength Emphasis (4–8 weeks)
- 2–3 lower and 2 upper exposures/week (often via total-body splits)
- Main lifts around 75–90% 1RM with controlled volumes
- Continue medicine ball + jump intent work

### Phase 3: Strength–Speed / Velocity Transfer (4–6 weeks)
- Keep one heavy exposure/week per pattern
- Increase ballistic work (jumps, Olympic derivatives if coached, med-ball throws)
- Higher throwing specificity and mound integration

### Phase 4: Preseason Bridge (2–4 weeks)
- Gradual volume drop, preserve intensity
- Tight coordination of throwing ramp, bullpen/live AB demands, and lifting
- Avoid “last-minute crush blocks” that produce residual fatigue at season start

## Exercise selection principles
- Prioritize high ROI patterns: squat/hinge, unilateral lower, horizontal & vertical pull, anti-rotation + rotation power.
- Include direct posterior cuff, external rotation, scap upward rotation, and serratus work.
- Maintain forearm/wrist/grip robustness for deceleration and elbow support.

## Off-season frequency guidelines (typical)
- Lifting: 3–4 days/week
- Power/ballistics: 2–4 touches/week
- Arm-care microdoses: 4–6 brief exposures/week
- Full rest day: at least 1/week

## Velocity development integration
- Gains are best when gym, throwing, mechanics, and recovery are coordinated.
- Weighted-ball and high-intent throwing can be useful tools, but only with progression and monitoring.

## Injury-risk reduction integration
- Address known risk factors (shoulder ER weakness/ROM issues, workload spikes, poor recovery).
- Build robust lower-half/trunk capacity to reduce over-reliance on distal arm structures.

## Common mistakes
- Chasing only PRs in bilateral lifts without transfer work
- Excessive conditioning that blunts power adaptations
- Copy-paste social media programs without context
- Skipping preseason transition and entering season with high residual fatigue
