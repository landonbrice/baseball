# 01 — Evidence Summary

## What was queried (explicit)
I queried studies using PubMed/NCBI endpoints (esearch/esummary/efetch) for terms including:
- "baseball pitcher resistance training velocity"
- "in-season strength training baseball pitchers"
- "weighted baseball injury risk"
- "baseball pitcher shoulder strength injury risk prospective"
- "throwing velocity resistance training baseball systematic review"
- "concurrent training interference power athletes meta-analysis"

I then reviewed key abstracts/systematic reviews plus applied sources (Driveline, Tread, MLB Pitch Smart).

## Bottom line (high confidence)
1. **Pitchers should generally lift in-season** (with reduced volume, controlled fatigue) to preserve neuromuscular qualities and reduce detraining.
2. **Velocity can improve from specific strength/power interventions** (resistance/plyometric/weighted-ball-adjacent methods), but program design quality matters.
3. **Injury risk is multifactorial**; no single lifting method “solves” risk. Workload management + shoulder function + adequate recovery are central.
4. **Preseason deficits (e.g., shoulder ER weakness/ROM issues) are associated with in-season injury risk**, supporting targeted prehab/strength work.

## What evidence is strongest
- **Prospective cohort injury-risk data** in baseball pitchers (e.g., shoulder strength/ROM risk factors).
- **Systematic reviews/meta-analyses** on youth/adolescent baseball arm injury risk and overhead throwing interventions.
- **Biomechanical weighted-ball studies** that clarify mechanism (velocity and arm-speed effects, mixed kinetic implications by protocol).

## What evidence is moderate/limited
- Direct head-to-head RCTs comparing full in-season lifting models (e.g., high-low vs undulating vs minimalist maintenance) in pitchers.
- Longitudinal injury outcomes for modern "velocity ecosystem" programs (weighted balls + mound + gym + recovery stack).

## Practical interpretation by claim

### Claim A: "Lifting in-season hurts performance"
**Not supported broadly.** Better supported approach is to lift with lower volume + strategic timing and avoid excessive soreness/fatigue near outings.
- Driveline applied pieces consistently advocate this model.
- Team-sport in-season data supports maintaining strength/power with concise doses.

### Claim B: "Weighted balls increase velo but always raise injury risk"
**Too absolute.** Evidence suggests weighted-ball interventions can improve velocity/arm speed, but risk appears sensitive to:
- athlete age/training age
- progression and load selection
- total throwing volume and recovery
- integration with strength and monitoring
Some studies/reviews report injury concern; others show kinetic neutrality in specific contexts.

### Claim C: "Off-season should be only maximal strength"
**Incomplete.** Best-supported models combine:
- force production (max strength)
- high-velocity intent (power/ballistics)
- tissue capacity/rotator cuff/scap function
- individualized throwing progression

## Evidence-informed hierarchy for coaches
1. Manage total stress (throwing + lifting + life/sleep).
2. Maintain or build force qualities with minimal effective dose in-season.
3. Keep high intent exposures (jumps/medball/sprint variants) year-round.
4. Monitor shoulder status and modify early.
5. Use weighted-ball methods only inside a full load-management system.
