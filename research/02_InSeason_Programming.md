# 02 — In-Season Programming (Pitchers)

## In-season objective hierarchy
1. **Be ready to compete at highest level each outing**
2. **Minimize detraining** of force/power qualities
3. **Control tissue stress** (elbow/shoulder) and cumulative fatigue
4. **Maintain movement quality** (ROM, cuff/scap function, lower-half contribution)

## Core principles

### 1) Reduce volume, not intent
- Keep intensity/intent reasonably high on key lifts and power work.
- Reduce total sets/reps and exercise menu vs off-season.
- Avoid grinding to failure; keep reps in reserve.

**Why:** applied baseball and broader in-season team sport literature support maintaining qualities with lower training volume.

### 2) Time heavy stress away from next appearance
- Starters: highest lift dose typically early post-start window.
- Relievers: use micro-doses and flexible day-to-day adjustments.

### 3) Keep “speed qualities” alive
- Low dose jumps, medicine-ball throws, and/or sprint work (if athlete tolerates).
- Avoid replacing all neural work with only rehab/mobility circuits.

### 4) Integrate cuff/scap work and ROM checks weekly
- ER/IR strength balance, scap control, posterior cuff and lower trap work.
- Address ROM drift quickly (especially GIRD/external rotation changes).

### 5) Match lifting dose to throwing/workload spikes
- High pitch count, high-stress inning, or multiple appearances in a short window = reduce non-essential lift volume.

## Suggested in-season loading guidelines
- Main lift exposures: 1–2 per week (starters), 1–3 micro exposures (relievers)
- RPE target: mostly 6–8 (avoid frequent 9–10)
- Total lower-body hard sets/week: often ~6–12 (individualized)
- Total upper push/pull hard sets/week: often ~6–10 (individualized)
- Cuff/scap/prehab: 2–4 touches/week (brief, consistent)

## Throwing-day integration (starter example)
- **D0 (start day):** recovery/mobility only
- **D+1:** heavier lower + moderate upper, tissue flush, cuff/scap
- **D+2:** power + moderate total-body
- **D+3:** lower stress, mobility/arm care, light neural touches
- **D+4/5/6:** taper toward next start based on calendar

## Red flags to deload immediately
- >2–3 mph unexplained velocity drop with elevated effort
- meaningful command degradation + persistent arm heaviness
- progressive shoulder/elbow pain trend
- major sleep deficit or travel stress accumulation

## Driveline-aligned points (in-season)
- Maintain strength/power in-season via reduced volume and fatigue management.
- High-volume low-load lifting can still create large fatigue; strategic intensity + lower volume may be superior.

## Tread-aligned points (in-season)
- Emphasis on consistency, patience, and avoiding chaotic program hopping.
- Practical implication: keep core training pillars stable; iterate edges, not whole system weekly.

## Where evidence is weaker
- Exact best split for every reliever role
- Optimal weekly hard-set ceilings by competition level
- Universal weighted-ball volume thresholds for “safe” in-season use
