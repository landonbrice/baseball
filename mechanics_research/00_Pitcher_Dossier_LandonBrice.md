## 2. The mechanical thesis (why this project exists)

**The two biggest keys for Landon are hip-to-shoulder separation and the arm spiral.**
Everything the agents return should be ranked by how directly it serves those two.

### 2.1 The fault chain (what's happening now)

1. **Leg lift — hips closed.** He starts closed. Good — there's coil potential here.
2. **Drop — good.** He loads down into the back hip and gets a good drop / sink.
3. **Drop → rotate transition fails.** He cannot convert that loaded drop into a proper
   **rotate** phase. The back hip should fire and rotate (internally rotate / "close")
   to drive the pelvis — instead it stalls.
4. **Back hip leaks.** Rather than the trail hip rotating powerfully and *closing through*,
   it **leaks energy** and never fully clears.
5. **Front hip swings open early.** The lead hip swings ~45° around and the **lead leg
   lands open** — the pelvis is already open at foot strike, so the separation window
   never opens.
6. **Shoulders fly open / trunk not neutral.** Because the front side flies open, he can't
   keep the **trunk neutral and closed while the hips rotate at landing** → he loses
   hip-shoulder separation.
7. **Push arm action.** With no stored separation and no stable front side to brace
   against, force can't **spiral up the kinetic chain into the arm** → he **pushes** the
   ball instead of whipping/spiraling it.

> One-line summary: *good drop, no rotate; back hip leaks while the front side flies open
> early; no separation, no brace, so the arm pushes instead of spiraling.*

### 2.2 The model (what he should move toward)

- **Glove and lead leg travel together** and **initiate rotation later** — keep the front
  side closed and connected longer so a real separation window opens.
- **Sequence:** back hip drive **→** lead leg block. The trail hip rotates/closes to drive
  the pelvis; the **lead leg blocks** (front leg posts and braces) to decelerate the
  pelvis, which **pulls the torso → shoulders → arm** in order. A *pull/whip*, not a push.
- **Net outcome:** restore the kinematic sequence (pelvis → trunk → arm), restore
  hip-shoulder separation, convert the push to a spiral/whip arm action, and finish with
  **release off the fingers**.

### 2.3 Cue priority for this pitcher

The Agent Briefs define four canonical cues. For Landon they rank:

| Priority | Cue | Why it's weighted here |
|----------|-----|------------------------|
| **1** | **Staying Closed** | Hip-shoulder separation, glove-side discipline, glove-and-lead-leg-together, delayed front side, trunk-neutral-while-hips-rotate. This is the headline fault. |
| **2** | **Hip Rotation** | Back-hip drive / fixing the leak, stopping the front hip from landing open, lead-hip timing, trail-leg drive into the block. The engine behind #1. |
| **3** | **Arm Path** | Convert push → spiral/whip; let the trunk pull the arm; scap load and layback off a *braced* front side. Downstream of #1 and #2. |
| **4** | **Release** | Off the fingers, finger drive, spin axis. A stated goal but secondary to the front-side fix. |

**Cross-cutting:** the **lead leg block** is the single most important mechanical feature
for Landon — it sits at the seam of cues #1 and #2 and is the thing that converts hip
drive into a braced front side that whips the arm. Agents should over-index on lead-leg-block
and front-side content. (Tread Athletics' front-side / lead-leg-block library is expected to
be the richest single source for him.)

## 4. What "good" looks like for the agents

A returned drill earns its place if it does at least one of:
- Teaches the front side to **stay closed / arrive late** (glove + lead leg together).
- Trains the **lead leg block / front-side brace**.
- Trains **back-hip rotation / clearing** without leaking.
- Restores **hip-shoulder separation** as a feel.
- Converts a **push** arm action into a **whip/spiral** off a stable front side.
- Trains **release off the fingers** (secondary).

Anything that doesn't touch one of those — skip it (see the anti-drift rule in the briefs).
