Fix the mechanical inefficiencies so you throw biomechanically correct and efficient — velocity is a byproduct of efficiency, and your flexor-pronator / medial-elbow history stays protected every step of the way.

The fault chain, in one breath Good drop→no rotate→back hip leaks→front side flies open early→no separation→push arm action.

Why this order

The three areas are ranked by your worst diagnostic self-scores: glove-side @ foot-strike = 1 hip–shoulder separation = 2 hip-explosion sequence = 2. So Staying Closed and Hip Rotation lead — they are the engine and the brace. Arm Path is downstream and comes last; it largely fixes itself once the front side holds.

Staying Closed

PRIORITY 1

Fault: front hip swings open early, lead leg lands open, shoulders fly open — the separation window never opens.

Target model: glove and lead leg travel together and rotate late; arrive closed and stacked at foot plant, then the lead leg posts to decelerate the pelvis so the trunk catches up.

▸ The laws behind Staying Closed
  1. Delay hips & shoulders opening while still moving toward home = stored torque = impulse at release. Fire the back hip, keep the front side closed, and you get more torque → more impulse. Your front side flies open early, so the elastic band never stretches. Bauer · Tips with Trev Ep 22 → Bauer · Separation Ep 21 →
  2. Separation peaks just after foot strike — decelerate the pelvis at foot plant so the torso catches up. The window opens at foot contact (MLB ~40–60°). Arrive closed at foot plant — the exact opposite of your current open-at-landing pattern. IJSPT kinematics study → Driveline · Cueing model →
  3. Bring the chest to the glove — swivel it to face the chest and stabilize over the front foot. Don't pull it. The glove is a fulcrum to rotate around, not a lever to pull open. Pulling the glove is the fastest way to throw the front side open early. Mustard · Swivel & Stabilize → Tread · Glove Arm →
  4. Separation needs anti-rotation core + ≥45° T-spine rotation — you can't FEEL it without the canister. Below 45° T-spine, the trunk fly-opens or steals motion from the low back. It's a physical prerequisite, not a willpower one. Cressey · T-spine mobility → Driveline · Stability & mechanics →
⚠ Don't overcorrect Don't force the hips through, and don't jam the glove. A glove-blocking "firm front side" that stops deceleration short kills shoulder rotational velocity and adds shoulder/neck stress — the lead LEG blocks, the glove STABILIZES. Driveline · Don't teach firm front side →

▸ The laws behind Hip Rotation
  1. The FRONT-leg block — not the back-leg push — predicts velocity. Peak stride-leg ground force during arm-cocking is the single best velocity predictor (r² ≈ 0.61); back-leg drive doesn't correlate. Re-aim all effort from "push the rubber" to "post the front leg." Driveline · Front & back leg force plates →
  2. The lead leg straightens in RESPONSE to back-hip rotation — you don't actively push or jam it. As the back hip rotates around the posted front hip, the front knee passively extends. Cue back-hip rotation into a planted front leg — never "straighten the front leg." Tread · Front leg mechanics → Tread · weight-room block list →
  3. Drive the back hip with glute/ham (pull the ground back like a sprinter) — not a quad push. Rotate, don't lunge. The pelvis drives the rotation into landing; reframing the unload as "pelvis drives, glute pulls" stops the leak AND lowers arm stress. Tread · Drive Leg Mechanics → Tread · Back hip into landing →
  4. Keep the back knee rolling downward to keep the hips "in play" until footstrike, so the hips LEAD the torso. If the back leg extends early, hips and torso rotate together and separation collapses. A concrete, filmable checkpoint on your 240fps camera — Bauer's own diagnostic of exactly your fault. Bauer · film breakdown →
⚠ Don't overcorrect Don't cue the back-leg push. The force-plate data is explicit — back-leg drive doesn't predict velocity, and cueing "push the rubber" re-creates the leak. Cue rotate around the front hip, not push off the back.

▸ The laws behind Arm Path / Spiral
  1. The TRUNK pulls the arm into layback off a braced front side — the arm spirals, it doesn't push. The trunk rotation pulls the arm from external into internal rotation; the arm is the end of the whip, never the initiator. Your push is downstream of Areas 1 & 2 — fix the front side and the spiral largely appears. Bauer + Yu Darvish · Arm Action →
  2. The #1 fix for a pushing arm action is keeping the shoulders CLOSED at landing. Lost hip-shoulder separation is central to pushing; so is injury-compensation (your elbow history is a cause — the brain won't whip into positions it doesn't trust). Keep balls light to remove that cause. Tread · 7 causes of pushing →
  3. Think PROXIMAL, relax DISTAL — drive the trunk / "pull with the pec or armpit," let the hand stay loose. A tense forearm is both a velocity leak AND a medial-elbow stressor. "Relax the hand, drive the armpit" is faster arm + safer elbow. Tread · The 3 Types of Muscling Up →
  4. Keep the arm IN the plane of shoulder rotation — don't finish straight down to "throw strikes." Over-flexing the torso drops the arm out of plane so the trunk can't rotate it through. Staying in-plane lets the trunk spiral the arm rather than push it forward. Tread · Sync the arm into the plane →
⚠ Don't overcorrect Don't cue or drill the arm directly first. The push is a symptom of no separation + no brace. Fix Staying Closed and Hip Rotation first — muscling the arm path in isolation (or "fake layback" via elbow flexion / heavy balls) just adds elbow stress without fixing the cause.

⚠ Global Guardrails

Year-1 conservative-load rules — non-negotiable. This is a healthy-build year: the flexor-pronator / medial-elbow history is protected, and velocity is the byproduct of efficiency, not the chase.

≤ 6oz on everything

No 7oz+ overload balls this year. Underload 3–4oz (never below 3oz) is the preferred arm-speed lane — moves the arm faster with less force than a 5oz ball.

No pulldowns / max-effort throws

No run-and-throw pulldowns. Deferred to Phase 6 minimum, gated on a clean elbow + a block that holds in a bullpen.

No max intent before the mound phase

Hybrid ~90% plyo days are Phase 6+. "Intent of purpose, not of effort" through Phase 5 — every catch-play throw is a mechanics rep.

Mechanics-efficiency BEFORE intent

Velocity is a byproduct. Fix the sequence first; chasing velo/spin before the front side holds just re-grooves the push.

FOREARM TIGHTNESS = STOP SIGN

Forearm tightness ends the session, every time. UCL-area sensation on elbow extension = back off. The pronator/FCU durability block runs year-round. Cressey · Mighty Elbows & Wrists → Cressey · Everything Elbow →

Common mistakes that re-create his faults

  • Cueing "push off the back leg / drive the rubber." Re-grooves the back-hip leak and early back-leg extension — the opposite of rotating around a posted front hip.
  • Pulling/yanking the glove to the hip to "open up." Throws the front side open early and kills the separation window — the chest should travel to the glove; the glove stays stable over the front foot.
  • Letting the pelvis arrive OPEN at foot strike. The separation window only exists at and just after landing — if he's already open, there's nothing to store.
  • Going to heavier balls / max intent to "feel more arm speed." With his elbow history, heavy balls and a tense forearm are causes of the push and a medial-elbow stressor. Underload + sub-max is the lane.
  • Drilling the arm path in isolation before the front side is braced. The whip has nothing to react against, so he muscles up the hand/forearm — re-creating the push and stressing the flexors.
