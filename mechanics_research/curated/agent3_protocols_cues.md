## A) HIP ROTATION
*back hip drives/clears INTO a posted lead-leg block — fault: good drop, no rotate, back hip leaks, he spins instead of sequencing.*

### The biggest laws / protocols

1. **The FRONT-leg block — not the back-leg push — predicts velocity.** Peak *stride-leg*
   ground reaction force during arm-cocking is the single best velocity predictor (r² ≈ 0.61);
   back-leg "drive" force does **not** significantly correlate.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* his instinct is to "push" off the back leg. The data says
     that's the wrong lever — his velocity is gated by a braced front side, which is exactly
     the feature he's missing. Re-aim all his effort from "push the rubber" to "post the front leg."
   - *Source:* Driveline, *Analyzing Front and Back Leg Ground Forces*
     (https://www.drivelinebaseball.com/2016/08/analyzing-front-and-back-leg-ground-forces-in-pitchers-using-force-plates/)

2. **The lead leg straightens in RESPONSE to back-hip rotation — you don't actively push or jam it.**
   As the back hip continues rotating *around* the posted front hip, the front knee passively
   extends. So you cue **back-hip rotation into a planted front leg**, never "straighten the
   front leg" or "push off the back leg."
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* his back hip *leaks* instead of rotating around a fixed front
     hip, so the block never forms. Train the rotate-around, and the block (and the velocity
     transfer) shows up on its own. This is the seam between his #1 and #2 faults.
   - *Source:* Tread, *Problem Solving Your Front Leg Mechanics*
     (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=J7SlDhs85n4); weight-room block list
     (https://x.com/TreadHQ/status/1883960310689857815)

3. **Drive the back hip with glute/hamstring (pull the ground back like a sprinter) — not a quad push.**
   Rotate, don't lunge. The pelvis (middle of the body) drives the rotation into landing — not
   the rear femur slamming into internal rotation.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* his good *drop* is wasted because the load never converts to a
     glute-driven *rotate*; reframing the unload as "pelvis drives, glute pulls" stops the leak
     AND lowers arm stress (protective for his elbow).
   - *Source:* Tread, *Drive Leg Mechanics to Increase Velocity & Reduce Arm Stress*
     (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1V5CmleRaoI); *Re-thinking How the Back Hip Works Into Landing*
     (https://www.tiktok.com/@tread_athletics/video/7429357491495963947)

4. **Keep the back knee rolling downward to keep the hips "in play" until footstrike, so the hips rotate and LEAD the torso.**
   If the back leg extends early, the hips and torso rotate together — separation collapses.
   Count the frames the hips are stopped while the torso keeps turning.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* this is Bauer's own film diagnostic of *exactly* Landon's
     fault (early back-leg disengage → hips + torso fly together). It gives a concrete, filmable
     checkpoint on his 240fps camera.
   - *Source:* Bauer film breakdown (https://x.com/BauerOutage/status/1163669225061388289)

### CUE WORDS (pitcher repeats)
- **"Rotate around the front hip"** (not push off the back)
- **"Pull the ground back"** (glute/sprinter drive, not quad)
- **"Hips lead, torso waits"**

## B) STAYING CLOSED
*hip-shoulder separation; glove + lead leg together; rotate LATE; trunk stays closed — fault: front side flies open early, no separation. (Landon's #1 priority.)*

### The biggest laws / protocols

1. **Delay the hips and shoulders opening while still moving toward home = stored torque = impulse at release.**
   "If you fire your back hip and keep the front side closed, you get more torque. The more
   torque, the more impulse at release." Separation is the stretched elastic band.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* his front side flies open early, so the band never stretches —
     he throws with zero stored torque. This is the headline fix and the headline *why*.
   - *Source:* Bauer, *How To Throw Harder | Tips with Trev Ep 22*
     (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l1dZ5z9dnXc); *Creating Shoulder/Hip Separation | Ep 21*
     (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVno6crh5u8)

2. **Separation peaks JUST AFTER foot strike — decelerate the pelvis immediately at foot plant so the torso catches up.**
   MLB average separation is ~40–60°. The window opens at foot contact; you rotate the hips
   explosively while *resisting* premature torso rotation, then the front side brakes and the
   torso accelerates to close the gap.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* his pelvis is already open *at* foot strike, so there's no
     window left to exploit. The fix is to arrive *closed* at foot plant — the exact opposite of
     his current pattern. Gives a filmable target (closed/stacked at landing → big separation).
   - *Source:* IJSPT kinematics study (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7727427/);
     Driveline, *A Different Model for Cueing Pitching Mechanics*
     (https://www.drivelinebaseball.com/2017/05/different-model-cueing-pitching-mechanics/)

3. **Glove side: bring the CHEST to the glove — swivel the glove to face the chest and STABILIZE over the front foot. Do not pull/yank it.**
   The torso moves to the glove, not the glove to the hip. The glove is a fulcrum to rotate
   *around*, not a lever to pull *open*.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* "shoulders fly open" is half a glove problem. Pulling the glove
     is one of the fastest ways to throw the front side open early. "Tuck and stabilize," don't
     "rip and pull." (This half of staying-closed is lighter in the drill library — over-cue it.)
   - *Source:* Mustard / Mustard-Ghost *Using the Glove: Swivel and Stabilize*
     (https://teammstrd.com/using-the-glove-swivel-and-stabilize-to-proper-position/);
     Tread, *Gain Velocity by Fixing Your Glove Arm* (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=maBIuUqxnx0)

4. **Separation is enabled by anti-rotation core stability + T-spine rotation — you can't FEEL it without the canister.**
   The pelvis rotates open under a *neutral, closed* trunk; that requires ribs-down rib-cage
   control and ≥45° thoracic rotation per side. Below 45° T-spine, the trunk fly-opens or steals
   motion from the low back.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* if his T-spine/rib-cage control is short, *no cue* will hold the
     trunk closed — it's a physical prerequisite, not a willpower one. Screen it before drilling.
   - *Source:* Cressey, *Improving Thoracic Mobility in Throwers*
     (https://ericcressey.com/thoracic-spine-mobility-drills-throwers/); Driveline,
     *Stability & Pitching Mechanics* (https://www.drivelinebaseball.com/2017/05/stability-pitching-mechanics/)

### CUE WORDS (pitcher repeats)
- **"Stay shut till you land"** (closed/stacked at foot plant)
- **"Chest to glove"** (not glove to hip)
- **"Hip leads, trunk waits"** (pelvis opens under a quiet trunk)

## C) ARM PATH / SPIRAL
*trunk WHIPS the arm off a braced front side — layback, in-plane, spiral not push — fault: push arm action. (Heavy Bauer.)*

### The biggest laws / protocols

1. **The TRUNK pulls the arm into layback off a braced front side — the arm spirals, it doesn't push.**
   Bauer/Darvish model: the trunk rotation pulls the arm from external rotation into internal
   rotation; the arm is the *end of the whip*, reacting to the trunk, never initiating.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* his push is *downstream* of B) and A). No braced front side →
     nothing for the trunk to whip against → the arm muscles up to compensate. Fix the front side
     and the spiral largely appears; cue the arm last.
   - *Source:* Bauer, *Arm Action Mechanics (Bauer + Yu Darvish)*
     (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3KyaEXCR-vg)

2. **The #1 fix for a pushing arm action is keeping the shoulders CLOSED at landing (separation), so the arm stays in line and can whip.**
   Of Tread's 7 causes of pushing, lost hip-shoulder separation is central; injury-compensation
   is another (directly relevant to Landon). Keep shoulders closed at landing → arm stays in the
   slot → it whips instead of pushing.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* confirms the arm path won't fix in isolation — it's a
     consequence of staying closed. Also names his elbow history as a *cause* of the push (the
     brain won't whip into positions it doesn't trust). Keeping balls light removes that cause.
   - *Source:* Tread, *How to Fix a Pushing Arm Action — 7 Causes and Solutions*
     (https://treadathletics.com/7-causes-of-pushing/)

3. **Think PROXIMAL, relax DISTAL — accelerate the trunk / "pull with the pec or armpit," and let the hand & forearm stay loose.**
   Muscling up the hand/forearm is "muscling up" — it slows the whip. Cue pulling down with the
   pec/lat reflexively; relax the end of the whip.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* a tense forearm is both a velocity leak AND a medial-elbow
     stressor. "Relax the hand, drive the armpit" is a two-for-one: faster arm + safer elbow.
   - *Source:* Tread, *The 3 Types of "Muscling Up"* (https://treadathletics.com/muscling-up/)

4. **Keep the arm IN the plane of shoulder rotation — don't finish straight down to "throw strikes."**
   When the torso over-flexes, the arm drops out of plane and the trunk can't rotate it through;
   staying in-plane lets the trunk spiral the arm rather than push it forward.
   - *Why it matters for Landon:* converts his out-of-plane push finish into a trunk-driven
     in-plane spiral. (Prerequisite: scapular upward-rotation/serratus capacity so the arm can
     lay back cleanly — train it concurrently in the weight room, off the throwing-side stress.)
   - *Source:* Tread, *How to Sync the Arm into the Plane of Shoulder Rotation*
     (https://treadathletics.com/plane-of-rotation/)

### CUE WORDS (pitcher repeats)
- **"Trunk whips the arm"** (arm reacts, never initiates)
- **"Relax the hand, drive the armpit"** (proximal over distal)
- **"Stay in plane / lay it back"** (in-plane spiral, not push)

## GLOBAL GUARDRAILS

### Year-1 conservative-load rules (non-negotiable — from `velocity_framework.md` / dossier)
- **≤6oz on everything.** No 7oz+ overload balls this year. Underload **3–4oz** (never below 3oz)
  is the *preferred* arm-speed lane — moves the arm faster with *less* force than a 5oz ball.
- **No pulldowns / run-and-throw max-effort throws.** Deferred to **Phase 6 minimum**, gated on a
  clean elbow + a block that holds in a bullpen.
- **No sustained max intent before the mound phase.** Hybrid A (~90%) plyo days are Phase 6+.
  "Intent of *purpose*, not of *effort*" through Phase 5 — every catch-play throw is a mechanics rep.
- **Mechanics-efficiency BEFORE intent.** Velocity is a byproduct here. Fix the sequence first;
  chasing velo/spin before the front side holds just re-grooves the push.
- **FOREARM TIGHTNESS = STOP SIGN.** Forearm tightness ends the session, every time. UCL-area
  sensation on elbow extension = back off. Pronator/FCU durability block runs year-round
  (*13 Tips for Mighty Elbows and Wrists* — https://ericcressey.com/13-tips-for-mighty-elbows-and-wrists/;
  *Everything Elbow* banded pronation/ulnar-deviation — https://ericcressey.com/everything-elbow/).

### The single biggest "don't overcorrect" per area
- **Hip Rotation — DON'T cue the back-leg push.** The force-plate data is explicit: back-leg drive
  doesn't predict velocity, and cueing "push the rubber" re-creates the leak. Cue *rotate around
  the front hip*, not *push off the back*.
- **Staying Closed — DON'T force the hips through, and DON'T jam the glove.** "Relax, don't force
  the hips through" (Tread). A glove-blocking "firm front side" that stops deceleration short
  *kills* shoulder rotational velocity and adds shoulder/neck stress — the lead LEG blocks, the
  glove STABILIZES. Block with the leg, not the glove.
  (Driveline, *Why We Don't Teach Equal and Opposite / Firm Front Side* —
  https://www.drivelinebaseball.com/2013/10/dont-teach-equal-opposite-firm-front-side/)
- **Arm Path — DON'T cue or drill the arm directly first.** The push is a *symptom* of no
  separation + no brace. Fix B) and A) first; muscling the arm path in isolation (or with heavy
  balls / "fake layback" via elbow flexion) just adds elbow stress without fixing the cause.

## COMMON MISTAKES that re-create his faults

- **Cueing "push off the back leg / drive the rubber."** Re-grooves the back-hip leak and the
  early back-leg extension — the opposite of rotating around a posted front hip.
- **Pulling/yanking the glove to the hip to "open up."** Throws the front side open early and
  kills the separation window — the chest should travel to the glove, glove stays stable over the front foot.
- **Letting the pelvis arrive OPEN at foot strike** (drifting/opening early). The separation
  window only exists *at and just after* landing — if he's already open, there's nothing to store.
- **Going to heavier balls / max intent to "feel more arm speed."** With his elbow history, heavy
  balls and a tense forearm are *causes* of the push (the brain won't whip into untrusted loads) —
  AND the medial-elbow stressor. Underload + sub-max is the lane.
- **Drilling the arm path in isolation before the front side is braced.** The whip has nothing to
  react against, so he muscles up the hand/forearm — re-creating the push and stressing the flexors.
