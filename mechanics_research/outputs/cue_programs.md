# Cue Programs — Landon Brice

Four cue-specific progressions compiled from the deduplicated drill library
(`drill_library_deduped.csv`). Sequenced by `progression_level` (1 → 5) with a single
cue word per week and equipment matched to phase:

- **Phase 3** = plyo balls / constraint work (no full-effort throwing)
- **Phase 4** = baseballs + light plyo / weighted (≤6oz)
- **Phase 5+** = baseballs primarily, pattern carried onto the mound

Cue priority for Landon (from `00_Pitcher_Dossier_LandonBrice.md`): **Staying Closed (P1) >
Hip Rotation (P2) > Arm Path (P3) > Release (P4)**. The **lead leg block** sits at the seam
of P1 and P2 and is the single most important feature, so it appears early and often in the
first two programs.

> **Conservative-load reminder:** all weighted/plyo work stays ≤6oz, sub-max, through Phase 5.
> Forearm tightness is the stop sign. See `velocity_framework.md` for the loading rules.

---

## 1. Staying Closed (P1) — *delay the front side; glove + lead leg together*

**Landon's fault:** the front hip swings open early and the lead leg lands open, so the
separation window never opens and the shoulders fly open with the hips. This program teaches
the trunk to stay neutral/closed while the pelvis rotates underneath it, and trains the lead
leg to **post and brace** rather than collapse. Start with rib-cage/T-spine control and a film
baseline, then constrain the lower half so the front side cannot fly open, then load the block.

| Week | Cue word | Drills (with source) | Equipment | Sets×Reps | Phase |
|------|----------|----------------------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | **"Ribs down"** | Ribs-Down Breathing Reset (Cressey); Side-Lying Extension-Rotation windmill (Cressey); *film baseline:* Breaking Down Your Mechanics — Tips w/ Trev Ep 1 (Bauer) | none/mat, 240fps camera | 2 x 5 breaths; 2 x 6-8/side; 1 film session | Phase 3 |
| 2 | **"Trunk waits"** | Pec Sequence — Top 3 Drills for PERFECT Mechanics (Bauer); 7 Drills To Increase Hip-To-Shoulder Separation (Tread) | plyo ball, band | 3 x 5; 7 drills x 4-5 | Phase 3 |
| 3 | **"Front side late"** | Problem Solving Your Front Leg Mechanics (Tread, diagnosis); Elevated Trail Leg Drill — Lead Leg Block Progression (Tread); Roll-In Throws (Driveline) | plyo ball, box, mound/flat | review + 3 x 5/side; 2 x 5 | Phase 3 |
| 4 | **"Glove stays"** | Gain Velocity by Fixing Your Glove Arm — 3 Glove Arm Drills (Tread); 6 Drills To Help Fix Your Front Leg (Tread) | glove, plyo ball, band, box | 3 drills x 5; 6 drills x 3-5 | Phase 3 |
| 5 | **"Stack and hold"** | How To Feel Hip-Shoulder Separation — band + counter-rotation (Tread); Pivot Pickoff Throw — separation/coil (Tread + Driveline + Bauer, sources_count 3) | band, plyo ball/baseball | 3 x 5/side; 8-10 throws | Phase 4 |
| 6 | **"Block then whip"** | Throw Harder by Mastering the Lead Leg Block [Full Routine] (Tread); Throw Harder By Mastering Hip-Shoulder Separation [Full Routine] (Tread) | plyo balls, band, baseball | follow routine | Phase 4 |

*Coaching note:* the lead-leg-block "Full Routine" (Week 6) is the destination — but
Cressey's **Pitching Mechanics: Is Lead Leg Blocking Enough?** warns it only transfers if
T-spine + scap upward rotation upstream are clean, which is why Weeks 1-2 front-load
rib-cage/T-spine work. Read **A Different Model for Cueing Pitching Mechanics** (Driveline)
for the external-cue framing.

## 2. Hip Rotation (P2) — *back hip drives, rotate late, into a posted front leg*

**Landon's fault:** good drop but no rotate — the back hip leaks energy and never clears, and
the drop→rotate transition stalls. This program isolates the **back-hip hinge/load**, trains
the trail hip to **drive and clear** (glute/ham, not quad push), and chains that drive into a
**braced front leg** so the pelvis decelerates and pulls the trunk. Lead-leg-block weight-room
builders run alongside the pattern work (they need no throwing volume, so they fit the
conservative year).

| Week | Cue word | Drills (with source) | Equipment | Sets×Reps | Phase |
|------|----------|----------------------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | **"Sit the hip"** | How to Feel the "Hinge" (Tread); Lead Hip Flexion — hamstring loaded (Tread weight-room); *screen:* Measuring Hip Internal Rotation 90/90 (Cressey) | none/light plyo, cable/band, table | 3 x 5; 3 x 8/side; 2 x each hip | Phase 3 |
| 2 | **"Anchor and coil"** | Drop Step Throws (Driveline); Step Back Drill (Driveline); Hip Airplanes (Tread weight-room) | plyo balls (450g/225g), band | 2 x 5; 2 x 5; 3 x 5/side | Phase 3 |
| 3 | **"Drive, don't push"** | Drive Leg Mechanics to Increase Velocity & Reduce Arm Stress (Tread); Static 45 Skier — Top 3 Drills (Bauer); Rotational KB Swing (Tread weight-room) | plyo ball, kettlebell, camera | review + 3 x 5; 3 x 6/side | Phase 3 |
| 4 | **"Rotate into the block"** | Fixing Your Lead Leg Block: Hip Rotation w/ Strap (Tread); Split-Stance MB Taps (Tread weight-room); 3D Strap Rotations (Tread weight-room) | strap/band, med ball | 3 x 5/side; 3 x 6/side; 3 x 5/plane | Phase 4 |
| 5 | **"Clear and post"** | Pivot Pickoff Throws — back-hip drive (Driveline + Tread + Bauer, sources_count 3); Mastering the Rocker Drill (Tread + Driveline, sources_count 2) | plyo balls (1kg/450g), wall | 2 x 5; 2 x 5 | Phase 4 |
| 6 | **"Posture squat / whole chain"** | Posture Squat — Top 3 Drills (Bauer, chains the drop→rotate); Walking Windup Drill (Tread + Driveline, sources_count 2) | plyo ball/baseball (225g) | 3 x 5; 2 x 5 | Phase 5 |

*Coaching note:* the evidence base is unusually clear here — **Analyzing Front and Back Leg
Ground Forces** (Driveline research, r²=0.45-0.61 for stride-leg force vs velocity, back-leg
"drive" not significant) and **Range of Motion, Hip-Shoulder Separation & Kinematics** (PMC
study) both say the **front-side brace**, not back-leg push, predicts velocity. Cue the back
hip to *clear into a post*, never to *push through*.

## 3. Arm Path (P3) — *let the trunk pull the arm; spiral, not push*

**Landon's fault:** with no stored separation and no stable front side, force never spirals up
the chain so he **pushes** the ball. This program is downstream of P1/P2 — it first builds the
**scap upward-rotation / cuff** capacity that lets the arm lay back cleanly, then grooves an
**in-plane whip** off the (now braced) front side. It deliberately starts at Phase 3 with
weight-room prerequisites so the throwing-side work can stay light.

| Week | Cue word | Drills (with source) | Equipment | Sets×Reps | Phase |
|------|----------|----------------------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | **"Scap up and out"** | Forearm Wall Slide with Band (Cressey); Serratus Wall Slide Variations (Cressey); Back-to-Wall Shoulder Flexion (Cressey) | band, wall | 2 x 8-10; 2 x 8/variation; 2 x 8 | Phase 3 |
| 2 | **"Quiet the push"** | How to Fix a Pushing Arm Action — 7 Causes (Tread, diagnosis); The 3 Types of "Muscling Up" (Tread) | camera, plyo balls | review + cue work | Phase 4 |
| 3 | **"Lay it back"** | Reverse Throws (Plyo) (Driveline + Bauer, sources_count 2); REAL Towel Drill / Holds / Up-Down-Out (Driveline) | plyo balls (450g/225g), towel, wall | 2 x 5; 3 x 8 | Phase 4 |
| 4 | **"Stay in plane"** | How to Sync the Arm into the Plane of Shoulder Rotation (Tread); Half-Kneeling Cable ER — Scapular Plane (Cressey) | plyo ball, cable, camera | 3 x 5; 3 x 10/arm | Phase 4 |
| 5 | **"Up and out front"** | Pivot Pickoff Throws (Plyo) — forearm/arm-path (Bauer + Tread + Driveline, sources_count 3); One-Arm Band Rotational Row from Low Setting (Cressey) | plyo balls (≤6oz), band | 2-3 x 5; 2 x 8/side | Phase 4 |
| 6 | **"Trunk whips the arm"** | Bauer + Yu Darvish Arm Action Mechanics (Bauer, model); Enhancing Scapular Dynamic Control (Cressey) | camera, band | review + 2 x 8-10 | Phase 5 |

*Coaching note:* keep all plyo ≤6oz here — Tread's **7 Causes of Pushing** explicitly lists
*too-heavy balls* and *injury compensation* as causes of a push, both directly relevant to
Landon's flexor-pronator history. The arm path won't fix in isolation; run this *concurrently*
with the Staying Closed / Hip Rotation programs, not before them.

## 4. Release (P4) — *finish off the fingers; stable spin axis*

**Landon's fault:** secondary. Release is a stated goal (slider/changeup shape, finger drive)
but it is downstream of the front-side fix — chasing spin axis before the separation/block
holds just re-grooves the push. This is the shortest program: conceptual grounding first, then
applied finger-drive/pitch-design work, all gated to Phase 5+ once the mechanics are stable.

| Week | Cue word | Drills (with source) | Equipment | Sets×Reps | Phase |
|------|----------|----------------------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | **"Know the axis"** | Spin Rate Part II: Spin Axis & Useful Spin (Driveline); SPIN AXIS EXPLAINED — Bauer's Arsenal (Bauer) | Rapsodo/Trackman, baseballs | reference + bullpen film | Phase 5 |
| 2 | **"Efficient spin"** | Mastering the Axis of Rotation — Spin Axis 3D (Driveline) | Rapsodo, baseballs | reference + catch reps | Phase 5 |
| 3 | **"Shape the slider"** | Optimizing Breaking Ball Shape — Data-Driven Pitch Design (Driveline) | Edgertronic/Trackman, baseballs | design session | Phase 5 |
| 4 | **"Drive the fingers"** | Trevor Bauer Pitch Grips (Bauer); Trevor Bauer Pitch Design (Bauer, high-speed release) | high-speed camera, Rapsodo, baseballs | grip + release reps | Phase 6 |

*Coaching note:* this program is intentionally thin and late — see the **Gaps** section. It is
education + measurement, not a drill ladder. Do not start it until the lead-leg block holds in
a bullpen.

## Gaps

**Quality-gate question:** *If a coach ran only these drills, in this sequence, for 18 weeks,
would Landon improve on hip-shoulder separation, the lead-leg block, and the push→spiral arm
action?*

**Verdict: Yes for the two priorities (separation + lead-leg block) and largely yes for the
push→spiral arm action — but with real gaps a coach must fill manually.**

What the library covers well:
- **Lead-leg block / front side** — deep and redundant across Tread, Driveline (the research
  base: *Efficient Front Leg Mechanics*, *Analyzing Front and Back Leg Ground Forces*),
  Bauer, and Cressey's strength prerequisites. This is the strongest part of the build, as
  expected.
- **Hip-shoulder separation** — strong: constraint drills, full routines, the kinematic-study
  evidence, and the T-spine/rib-cage prerequisites that make it physically possible.
- **Back-hip drive without leak** — well covered (Drive Leg Mechanics, Hinge, Drop Step, KB swing).

Gaps to name explicitly:

1. **No on-mound / transfer ladder (Layer 4).** The library has exactly **one** Layer-4
   Transfer row (Bauer's *Preparing For a Start*). There is no structured bullpen progression,
   no flat-ground→mound transfer plan, no "carry the cue into a 60% then 75% then game pen"
   ladder. Without this, the drill-built pattern risks not surviving contact with a real
   delivery (the dossier's exact worry: reverting to the push in a start). **A coach must build
   the bullpen transfer phase manually.** This is the biggest gap.

2. **Release (P4) content is thin and conceptual.** Four rows, all education/pitch-design
   articles — zero release-specific *drills* (no finger-pronation throws, no axis-feedback
   catch drills, no wall-pronation reps). Adequate as a "not now, low priority" tier, but if
   release becomes a focus a coach will need to source actual finger-drive drills.

3. **No objective separation/feedback loop wired in.** The diagnostics exist (Seated Trunk
   Rotation Test, 90/90 Hip IR, Bauer's 240fps film workflow, biomech-report framing) but
   there's no prescribed re-test cadence. The 18-week plan should re-screen T-spine rotation
   and lead-leg HIR at weeks 6/12/18 and re-film at the same checkpoints — that scheduling is
   not in the library.

4. **Glove-side discipline is light vs. the lead-leg coverage.** One dedicated drill (Tread's
   3 Glove Arm Drills) plus Bauer's upper-half video. Given Landon's "shoulders fly open"
   fault, one more glove/front-shoulder constraint drill per week would help; the library
   leans on the *leg* block and slightly under-serves the *glove/trunk* half of "staying
   closed." Driveline's *Why We Don't Teach Equal and Opposite* is the right nuance reference
   so the fix doesn't overcorrect into a glove jam.

5. **Programming concurrency, not sequence, is the real model.** These four programs are
   written as parallel tracks but presented sequentially. For 18 weeks a coach should run
   Staying Closed + Hip Rotation together (weeks 1-8), layer Arm Path concurrently from ~week 3,
   and hold Release to weeks 12+. Running them strictly one-after-another would waste the
   front-loaded prerequisite work.

**Net:** the separation + lead-leg-block + push→spiral fixes are well-served by the *pattern*
and *prerequisite* layers; the build's weak point is the **transfer layer onto the mound** and
a thin **release** tier. Fill those two and the 18-week plan clears the gate.
